Overview

The simulation efforts carried out by the University of Tennessee’s nuclear engineering department in support of this research consist of detailed energy deposition modeling with the GEANT4 toolkit to understand the mechanisms and optimize film thickness along with MCNPX calculations for large scale detector design and performance.   Two example simulation projects are described; one in the optimization of the RPM8 with genetic algorithms and another using the simulated energy deposition to calculate the optimal film thickness.

 

Energy Deposition Simulation

Neutron detectors often utilize a material doped with an isotope of a large thermal cross section for absorption, such as 3He, 6Li or 10B. A possible replacement technology being investigated is 6Li (thermal cross section of 940 barns) embedded in a scintillating polymer matrix. The difference in the energy deposition in the material from charged particles (from the products of a neutron absorption) and from photon interactions introduces an opportunity to exploit the difference in energy deposition in order to maximize the discrimination between neutron and photon interactions in a detector.

 

The energy deposition of neutrons and gammas was investigated for polymeric films containing 6LiF of thickness ranging from 15 microns to 1 cm. The pulse height spectra of these samples is measured on site from a moderated neutron source and a 60Co source. Simulations were completed using GEANT4, a Monte Carlo transport toolkit [3,4]. GEANT4 was chosen for its ability to perform detailed tracking of the secondary electrons produced in a cascade, as well as its ability for light transport (a future aim of this project). The measurements were then used to validate and bench mark the simulation.

 

Detector Simulation

 

The detector geometry was simulated as a single layer of neutron absorbing material mounted atop of a non-scintillating material. The initial events where chosen by setting up a particle gun for 0.025 eV neutrons upon the detector and for both gammas resulting from a 60Co decay. The user of the GEANT4 toolkit is responsible for selecting the proper physics processes to model. Thus, extensive use of G4ModularPhysicsList was employed to handle the assigning of the physics processes to each particle in the correct order. The physics lists chosen for this simulation are listed below:

·       G4EmStandardPhysics Electromagnetic physics defines the lepton processes of multiple scattering, electron ionization, and electron bremsstrahlung.

·       G4EmLivermorePhysics Livermore physics extends EMStandardPhyiscs down to low (250 eV) energies based on the Livermore data sets. The processes extended are the photoelectric effect, Compton scattering, Rayleigh scattering, gamma conversion, ionization and bremsstrahlung.

·       HadronPhysicsQGSP_BERT_HP Hadronic physics are included to model the nuclear interactions.  Once a hadron has an energy of 20 MeV the high precision, ENDFB/VI data driven cross section models are applied.

·       G4IonPhysics Finally, to handle the transport of the charged ions resulting from an 6Li(n,α)3He interaction the  G4IonPhysics list was used.

 

RESULTS AND AnalsyIS

 

It is well established that the pulse height of a radiation event is proportional to the energy deposition of the event. Thus, the validity of the GEANT4 simulation can be determined by comparing the spectra shapes of measured spectra to simulated energy deposition. This was quantified by computing the average energy deposition and the average pulse height. In general, good agreement was observed. The energy deposition for neutrons and gammas was calculated in thin polymeric films of thickness from 15 microns to 1 cm with the GEANT4 toolkit and compared to the measured spectra. Layered Detector Simulations

 

 

Genetic algorithms provide a search method analogous to biological evaluation. Rather than following a gradient of a response function, genetic algorithms generate possible hypothesis by repeatedly applying genetic operators (mutation and recombination) to the best currently known hypothesis for the generation of subsequent populations of hypothesis. In this way the search space of candidate hypothesis (possible detector designs) is searched to identify the best hypothesis; the design that uses the least amount of 6Li while meeting the criteria. The genetic algorithm typically consist of four tasks: creating an initial population, evaluating that populations fitness, selecting members of the current population to breed, and then applying genetic operators to the selected members to breed the new population. This is completed until either a maximum generation is reached or the desired fitness is achieved.  For this problem the geometry was enumerated as a finite combination of absorber and moderating layers, represented as a bit string representation in which the geometry can be thought as a sequence of moderator (indicated by a 0) and detector layers (indicated by a 1).

 

The optimal geometry found by the genetic algorithm are shown in Table 1, with an MCNP rendering in Figure1 for a 26 length genome.  As genetic algorithms have a random component, they will often not converge to exactly the same result, as shown by the repeated genomes of length 13 in Table 1. Proper placement of the neutron absorbing layers can greatly reduce the number of layers needed. It is necessary to thermalize the flux in order to effectively utilize the high thermal cross section of 6Li. After a detector layer the thermal flux will have been significantly depleted, and thus it is necessary to moderate the neutron flux before another detector layer. A simple repeating layer design does not allow for the efficient usage of the neutron absorber. For example, it is necessary to build up the thermal flux with moderation in order to effectively utilize the large thermal cross section of 6Li, making a strictly alternating layered design inefficient. After the populations converged, it was found that a significant reduction in the number of layers is possible.
