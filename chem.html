Scintillation Material Synthesis

 Back to Top

Part of this project involves the fabrication and characterization of new materials that are capable of radation detection. These efforts are divided into polymer chemistry in which a neutron absorber is incorporated into a polymer to make a composite material, composite polymer fibers, and amorphous glass materials. These research efforts are summerized below, with some prelimary results given in the Radiation Characterization section.

Polystryene Systems

Andrew Mabe

It is of interest to the radiation detection community to develop polymeric materials capable of detecting thermal energy neutrons in the presence of strong gamma-ray fields. Lithium-6 dispersed in various organic polymers containing fluorescent compounds have been designed, synthesized, fabricated as thin films for this purpose. Three main types of polymers containing lithium-6 in the form of either mechanical mixtures of inorganic compounds in the polymer or dispersed atomically in various polymer matrices have been investigated. The first is polystyrene containing 6LiF and fluors 2,5-diphenyloxazole and 1,4-bis(5-phenyloxazol-2-yl)benzene in which the 6LiF particles exist as a mechanical mixture in the polymer matrix, which ultimately results in an opaque scintillator. The second is poly(styrene- lithium maleate) containing salicylic acid as the fluor. In this material, lithium is atomically dispersed in the polymer matrix which results in optical transparency up to 3.91 wt% 6Li. The third is poly styrene-co-lithium maleate-co-2-phenyl-5-(4-vinylphenyl) oxazole This material also exhibits improved transparency over polymer/lithium salt composites. A variety of chemical and physical characterizations as well as optical and scintillation characterizations have been performed to guide the development of optimized compositions of each type of polymer film.

Composite Polymer Films

Rohit Uppal

Back to Top

Neutron absorber particles were synthesized indigenously. Neutron absorber particles were ball milled. Polymer, ground neutron absorber particles and a fluor were mixed in desired ratios. The mixtures were weighed and formed into films on a compression-molding machine (Figure 1). The composite polymer films (CPF) were post-processed. Circular samples of diameter 50mm were cut for characterization for their radiation responses from 60Co and 0.59 µg 252Cf sources and compared to that of a cerium activated lithium silicate glass scintillation disc (GS20) using a PMT at identical conditions on an indigenously developed setup. Neutron/Gamma response of composite PEN film and GS20 are given in figure 2.

In order to meet the criteria of intrinsic gamma-neutron detection efficiency and neutron-gamma discrimination, a lower level discriminator (LLD) was set at channel number to provide the discrimination between neutron and photons, at which the gamma intrinsic efficiency drops to less than 10-6 (Figure 2). The neutron count rate above the LLD for CPF was found to be 19 cps. Neutron-gamma discrimination could be established based on pulse height (Figure 2). Considering 6250 photons per MeV for GS20, the relative photons per neutron MeV based on the end point for the peak for the best CPF was found to be 5183 photons.

Amorphous Glasses

John Auxier II

Back to Top

Most neutron detectors rely upon the use of a single isotope, such as 6Li, 10B, 155Gd, or 157Gd instead of incorporating multiple neutron-sensitive isotopes into the same matrix. Recently, it has been of interest to develop materials that incorporate multiple neutron-sensitive isotopes to increase the neutron detection sensitivity. Some groups have attempted to use lithium borate phosphate glasses that are doped with Ce3+, and have reported limited success with neutron/gamma discrimination. Lithium tetraborate (Li2B4O7:Ce) has been reported in the literature for use as a thermal neutron detector, however there are reports of the polycrystalline material. This material lends itself to the problem of neutron detection given the high loading of Li and B per each molecule.This material also has high optical transparency in the wavelength range of interest, and unlike many detector materials, it is not hygroscopic and is highly stable across a wide range of temperatures.

To address this problem, this project will investigate the usefulness of lithium tetraborate doped with Ce to address the problem of thermal neutron detection. The focus of this work will be three-fold:

  • to characterize the elemental composition morphology of Ce doped amorphous lithium tetraborate,
  • to determine the effect of boric acid on the reduction of rare earths (e.g.Ce) in a borate matrix, and
  • to determine the feasibility of using this material to detect thermal neutrons while meeting the DHS criteria.

Fibers

 Back to Top

Stephen Young

Organic polymeric materials as scintillators can be machined into a wide variety of shapes, including thin films and thin fibers, and have been demonstrated to be effective for thermal neutron detection due to their high light yield, low Z, and short decay time[1-4]. The 6Li isotope has a significant thermal neutron cross-section and produces high-energy charged particles upon thermal neutron absorption. There is a growing interest in small-diameter fibers (micro-/nanoscale) due to its 1-D structure as a waveguide, where light is emitted isotropically and guided along the long axis of the fiber by total internal reflection, exhibiting interesting optical properties such as optical confinement[5,6]. 6Li loaded polymeric small diameter fibers have been melt-spun and tested for thermal neutron detection. Our hypothesis is by incorporating 6Li into a polymeric scintillating matrix, following neutron capture event, the energy will be transferred along the fibers where emitted photons are detected on a standard photomultiplier tube.

Melt-spinning is a process where a polymer melt is extruded through a spinneret positioned vertically where the filament is cooled and collected onto a take-up winder as shown below[7].

The benefit of melt-spinning is that fibers can be fabricated without use of hazardous solvent and collected at high speeds that can increase crystallinity and mechanical properties of scintillating fibers. In this research study, 6LiF polymeric scintillation fibers were fabricated using a melt-spinning technique to produce uniaxial fibers (following two images) that directly discriminate between neutron and gamma radiation.

LiF is attractive due to its high atomic mass density of 6Li, is nonhygroscopic and thermally stable at elevated temperatures[8]. The next two images show example 6LiF loaded polymeric fibers (LiF Polymer) encapsulated in optical dielectric gel and its exposure to UV.

An example net thermal neutron response for these fibers compared to commercial lithiated glass scintillator, GS20, is shown below. The initial results are encouraging given that good neutron-gamma discrimination could be clearly observed. Future research will include optimizing the light collection geometry of fibers and investigating the scintillation properties of fibers for various loadings of 6LiF.
