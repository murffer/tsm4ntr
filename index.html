Project Description

The goals of this project are to:

  • develop new scintillation materials in amorphous and crystalline form that have a transformational impact on neutron and gamma-ray detection technology

  • fabricate and test detectors with various novel scintillation materials in conjunction with position sensitive photomultiplier tubes and EM-charge coupled devices,

  • develop suitable numerical models for energy deposition in detectors and calculate response functions, and develop much needed ability to predict the performance of new detection systems with respect to the probability of false alarms,

  • provide unique training opportunities for both undergraduate and graduate students as well as postgraduate researchers in the development of relevant scintillation materials, their characterization, and testing including detector evaluation with integrated electronics, and

  • develop and implement new undergraduate and graduate courses for radiation methodology that will be made available via distance education leading to a nationwide impact.

Several polymeric composite scintillation materials are synthesized and fabricated into thin film detectors for neutrons that satisfy Department of Homeland Security performance characteristics as determined by measurements and simulations for the experimental and computational configurations utilized. The intrinsic efficiency for Co-60 gamma rays is determined by a measurement in a 10 mR/hr field, and the absolute neutron absorption efficiency is based on results from Monte Carlo calculations for a layered detector model with the same footprint as utilized by field-deployed radiation portal monitors. A genetic algorithm is used to optimize the spacing of absorbing layers and the amount of moderating material between layers. Measurements and calculations for light transport in layered configurations are accomplished, and more extensive comparisons are underway.

Single crystals for the efficient detection of neutrons and photons have been grown and tested, and new Ce3+ and Eu2+ single crystal compositions have excellent response for neutrons and gamma rays, and inorganic scintillator powders have been incorporated into polymeric matrices.

New courses in nuclear threat detection and in radiation detection and measurement are developed and taught. Students from South Carolina State University (SCSU, an HBCU) have completed a hands-on training workshop and internships, and significant collaborative research is accomplished with SCSU personnel.

Recent Awards

The members of this groups won the 2013 Chancellor's Honors Award for Multidisplicnary Research.
