Amorphous Organic Materials (Andrew Mabe)

It is of interest to the radiation detection community to develop polymeric materials capable of detecting thermal energy neutrons in the presence of strong gamma-ray fields. Lithium-6 dispersed in various organic polymers containing fluorescent compounds have been designed, synthesized, fabricated as thin films for this purpose. Three main types of polymers containing lithium-6 in the form of either mechanical mixtures of inorganic compounds in the polymer or dispersed atomically in various polymer matrices have been investigated. The first is polystyrene containing 6LiF and fluors 2,5-diphenyloxazole and 1,4-bis(5-phenyloxazol-2-yl)benzene in which the 6LiF particles exist as a mechanical mixture in the polymer matrix, which ultimately results in an opaque scintillator. The second is poly(styrene-co-lithium maleate) containing salicylic acid as the fluor. In this material, lithium is atomically dispersed in the polymer matrix which results in optical transparency up to 3.91 wt% 6Li. The third is poly[styrene-co-lithium maleate-co-2-phenyl-5-(4-vinylphenyl)oxazole]. This material also exhibits improved transparency over polymer/lithium salt composites. A variety of chemical and physical characterizations as well as optical and scintillation characterizations have been performed to guide the development of optimized compositions of each type of polymer film.

Amorphous Inorganic Materials (John Auxier II)

Most neutron detectors rely upon the use of a single isotope, such as 6Li, 10B, 155Gd, or 157Gd instead of incorporating multiple neutron-sensitive isotopes into the same matrix.  Recently, it has been of interest to develop materials that incorporate multiple neutron-sensitive isotopes to increase the neutron detection sensitivity.  Some groups have attempted to use lithium borate phosphate glasses that are doped with Ce3+, and have reported limited success with neutron/gamma discrimination. Lithium tetraborate (Li2B4O7:Ce) has been reported in the literature for use as a thermal neutron detector, however there are reports of the polycrystalline material.  This material lends itself to the problem of neutron detection given the high loading of Li and B per each molecule.  This material also has high optical transparency in the wavelength range of interest, and unlike many detector materials, it is not hygroscopic and is highly stable across a wide range of temperatures.

Image of a fabricated Lithium Tetraborate Sample

To address this problem, this project will investigate the usefulness of lithium tetraborate doped with Ce to address the problem of thermal neutron detection.  The focus of this work will be three-fold: 1) to characterize the elemental composition morphology of Ce doped amorphous lithium tetraborate, 2) to determine the effect of boric acid on the reduction of rare earths (e.g. Ce) in a borate matrix, and 3) to determine the feasibility of using this material to detect thermal neutrons while meeting the DHS criteria.
